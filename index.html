The visa threshold is real. It exists for a reason.

Thailand's retirement visa requires you to be 50 years of age or older, with either 800,000 THB in a Thai bank account (about $22,000 USD) or roughly $2,200/month in guaranteed income. If you're under 50, this specific pathway isn't available to you yet. If you're over 50 but the finances aren't quite there, we talk about it honestly on the first call, before anyone books a flight. Coming here without enough money is a problem I've seen play out badly, and I won't set you up for that.

⚖️

A note on who is actually allowed to do this.

You will find plenty of Western expats advertising relocation and concierge services in Thailand on social media. Most of them cannot legally work here. Thailand's work permit laws are strict and operating without one is a real risk for them and potentially for you. As a Thai citizen, I operate this business legally, openly, and permanently. Where We Work

Bangkok and Chiang Mai.

Most people picture Thailand as beaches. The beach is a cheap flight away whenever you want it. Where you actually live is a different question, and it has more to do with who you are than what looks good in photos.

🏙️

Bangkok

A city of ten million where you can eat a proper meal for two dollars, take the train across town for a dollar, and live in a quiet neighbourhood that most visitors never find. The transit system keeps expanding. The further you get from the expat areas, the cheaper and more interesting things get. History and temples turn up on streets that aren't in any guidebook.

  • Growing BTS and MRT network including newer lines
  • World-class private hospitals
  • Residential neighbourhoods at every price point
  • History and culture on streets most tourists never find
  • Higher cost than Chiang Mai, still a fraction of home
🌿

Chiang Mai

Slower, cooler, and cheaper than Bangkok. You can drive yourself around without it being a nightmare. The Old City has temples that are over 700 years old. Older than anything in Bangkok. A large expat community has settled here, not because there's nothing else, but because once you've spent time here it's hard to imagine being somewhere else.

  • Lower cost of living than Bangkok
  • Manageable to drive or ride a scooter
  • Large, well-established expat community
  • Excellent hospitals including Bangkok Hospital Chiang Mai
  • Cooler climate, surrounded by mountains
The Numbers

What a good life here costs per month.

The table tells most of the story. The number that gets people isn't the rent. It's healthcare. A solid expat health insurance plan runs $80 to $120 a month. A GP visit costs $25. Private hospitals here are genuinely good. You'll see one on the scouting trip and judge for yourself.

Dental care isn't covered by Medicare at home. A cleaning at a good private clinic in Bangkok costs about $35. Not a deal. Just the price.

Thailand is affordable, not free. Coming here without enough money doesn't work. I've seen it happen and it doesn't end well. The retirement visa income threshold exists for exactly this reason. If you're not there yet, we talk about that before anyone buys a plane ticket.
